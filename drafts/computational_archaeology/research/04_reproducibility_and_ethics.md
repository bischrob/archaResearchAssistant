## Synthesized Notes

Reproducibility, open science, ethics, and responsible computational methods in archaeology are tightly linked to how data—especially Indigenous data—are governed and used.

**Reproducibility and responsible digital methods**

- Using digital or computational tools that the researcher does not understand, or methods whose analytic processes they cannot explain, is described as “inherently unethical.” This is framed as “not ethical scholarship.” [C7]  
  → For reproducible and ethical work, archaeologists must be able to understand, explain, and transparently document their computational workflows so others can assess and reproduce them. [C7]

- Archaeological data now move from paper and material archives into digital curation, which raises new ethical questions, especially where these data relate to living peoples. [C7]  
  → Reproducible workflows must therefore also account for how digital data are stored, accessed, and related back to descendant and Indigenous communities. [C7]

**Open science, FAIR, and the need for CARE**

- The current movement toward open data and open science is said not to “fully engage with Indigenous Peoples’ rights and interests.” [C1]  
  → Standard open-data logics can conflict with Indigenous rights if openness is prioritized without attention to power and history. [C1]

- Existing open data principles such as FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) focus on technical characteristics that facilitate sharing but “ignore power differentials and historical contexts.” [C1]  

- In archaeology, the FAIR principles are promoted as a framework to improve data management and reuse, but their implementation “must consider the CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, and Ethics).” [C9]  

- Applying FAIR together with CARE (“FAIR + CARE”) is proposed as a way to manage archaeological data throughout the data life cycle “in an ethically responsible manner,” especially given the complex technical, social, cultural, legal, and policy entanglements of archaeological data. [C8][C9]  

**CARE Principles and Indigenous data sovereignty**

- Indigenous data include data embedded in languages, knowledge, practices, technologies, natural resources, and territories, and also data collected by governments and institutions about Indigenous peoples and their lands. Such data are “intrinsic” to Indigenous peoples’ ability to realize their human rights and responsibilities. [C5]

- Indigenous data sovereignty reinforces Indigenous rights to decision-making according to Indigenous values and collective interests. [C5]

- The CARE Principles are explicitly designed as a people- and purpose-oriented complement to FAIR, encouraging open data movements to consider both people and purpose. [C1]  

Key CARE components for archaeological and computational practice:

1. **Collective Benefit**  
   - Data ecosystems should be designed so Indigenous peoples “derive benefit from the data,” and any value created should “benefit Indigenous communities in an equitable manner” and support their aspirations for wellbeing. [C2][C6]  
   - Governments and institutions should support Indigenous nations’ use and reuse of data to enable Indigenous innovation and self-determined development processes. [C6]  

2. **Authority to Control**  
   - Indigenous peoples’ rights and interests in Indigenous data must be recognized and “their authority to control such data be empowered.” [C2]  
   - They have rights to free, prior, and informed consent in collection and use of data, including policy and protocol development. [C2]  
   - They have the right to data relevant to their worldviews that empower self-determination and governance, and such data “must be made available and accessible” to support Indigenous governance. [C2][C3]  
   - They have the right to develop cultural governance protocols for Indigenous data and to be active leaders in stewardship and access, especially for Indigenous knowledge. [C3]  

3. **Responsibility**  
   - Those working with Indigenous data are responsible for showing how data use supports Indigenous self-determination and collective benefit and for providing meaningful, openly available evidence of these efforts. [C3]  
   - Use of Indigenous data is “unviable” unless linked to relationships based on respect, reciprocity, trust, and mutual understanding, as defined by the Indigenous peoples concerned. [C3]  
   - Researchers must ensure that creation, interpretation, and use of data uphold or respect the dignity of Indigenous nations and communities. [C3][C4]  
   - There is a reciprocal responsibility to enhance data literacy in Indigenous communities and to support Indigenous data workforce and digital infrastructure, including for creation, management, security, and governance of data. [C4]  
   - Resources must be provided to generate data grounded in Indigenous languages, worldviews, and lived experiences. [C4]  

4. **Ethics**  
   - Indigenous peoples’ rights and wellbeing should be the primary concern at all stages of the data life cycle and across the data ecosystem. [C4]  
   - Ethical data are those that do not stigmatize or portray Indigenous peoples, cultures, or knowledges in terms of deficit, and that align with Indigenous ethical frameworks and rights affirmed in UNDRIP. [C4][C12]  
   - Assessment of ethical benefits and harms should be done from the perspective of the Indigenous communities to whom the data relate. [C12]  
   - Ethical processes should address power and resource imbalances and include representation from relevant Indigenous communities. [C12]  
   - Data governance must consider potential future uses and harms, and metadata should acknowledge provenance, purpose, and any limitations or obligations in secondary use, including consent. [C12]  

**Computational methods, 3D, and emerging technologies**

- Advancements in imaging, laser scanning, drones, and related 3D workflows, along with uses such as replication, AI, VR/AR/MR/XR, offer opportunities for Indigenous cultural heritage but must be “understood and aligned with ethical considerations” to ensure responsible implementation. [C11]  

- Guidance on use, careful preservation, and attention to sovereignty, authority, and data protection must balance technical and methodological expertise *before* heritage data are broadly shared, in order to prevent inappropriate dissemination and potential harm. [C11]  

**Ethics, teaching, and future practice**

- It is false to assume that the same ethical practices automatically carry over from “trowel-to-ground excavations” or face-to-face ethnography into digital contexts; there are “additional issues to consider in the digital.” [C7]  

- Indigenous peoples can be especially harmed when colonizer-created data are distributed online and stored in inaccessible repositories beyond their control. [C7]  

- Teaching digital archaeology without “accompanying training on the ethical” dimensions is identified as a problem with serious implications for the future of the discipline. [C7]  

**Implications for reproducible and open computational archaeology**

Based on the above, ethically responsible and reproducible computational methods in archaeology should:

- Be technically transparent and explainable by their users. [C7]  
- Implement FAIR data practices in tandem with CARE, ensuring that openness and reuse are balanced with collective benefit, authority to control, responsibility, and ethics for Indigenous data. [C1][C8][C9]  
- Embed Indigenous data sovereignty: recognizing rights to self-determination, free prior and informed consent, governance protocols, and community-led decisions about access and reuse. [C2][C3][C5]  
- Anticipate future uses and potential harms of digital and computational products (including 3D and AI) and document limitations, obligations, and consent in metadata and governance frameworks. [C11][C12]  
- Prioritize relationships, capacity building, and benefit-sharing with Indigenous communities as core parts of digital and computational research design, not as add-ons. [C3][C4][C6]

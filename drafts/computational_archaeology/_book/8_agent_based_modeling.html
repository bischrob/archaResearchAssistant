10 8. Agent-Based Modeling and Simulation

10.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Explain why simulation models must be built from clearly defined objectives and explicit system theories.(Sargent 2013)
  • Distinguish between a “conceptual model” and a “computerized model” in simulation work.(Sargent 2013)
  • Describe the main steps in a rigorous simulation modeling workflow: conceptual model development, implementation, verification, and validation.(Sargent 2013)
  • Relate simulation model development to archaeological questions about material culture and social organization, such as ceramic grouping and exchange systems.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • Identify common pitfalls in building and evaluating simulation models, especially around data quality, group structure, and validity of inferences.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

10.2 Core Ideas

10.2.1 Simulation and System Theories

Simulation models are explicit representations of systems that are built to answer specific questions or meet clearly defined objectives.(Sargent 2013) These models are grounded in “system theories”—formal or informal understandings of how a real-world system works.(Sargent 2013) Developing valid theories typically requires multiple experiments or empirical studies on the real system.(Sargent 2013)

In computational archaeology, the “system” might be:

  • A production and distribution network of ceramics or ornaments.
  • The spatial and temporal structure of compositional groups.
  • Social or economic processes that might explain observed clusters in chemical or stylistic data.

Cluster analysis of Parowan Valley pottery and ornaments, using algorithms such as Ward’s method and average-linkage, is one way archaeologists explore the structure of such systems.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006) A simulation model would go a step further: not just describing clusters, but explicitly encoding rules or processes that could generate them.

10.2.2 Conceptual vs. Computerized Models

A core distinction in simulation work is between the conceptual model and the computerized model.(Sargent 2013)

  • The conceptual model is a mathematical, logical, or graphical representation that mimics the target system, built for the specific objectives of the study.(Sargent 2013)
  • The computerized model is the conceptual model implemented and running on a computer so that experiments can be conducted.(Sargent 2013)

In practice:

  • Conceptual model: verbal rules about how households might choose clays, tempers, or exchange partners; a diagram of sites and flows; or equations governing interaction.
  • Computerized model: the same logic implemented in code so you can simulate many runs and generate synthetic “ceramic data sets” or “exchange networks” for comparison to real data.

A written simulation model specification documents the design needed to implement the conceptual model in software.(Sargent 2013)

10.2.3 Objectives and Data

A simulation model should only be developed for a set of well-defined objectives.(Sargent 2013) This means you specify:

  • What archaeological pattern you are trying to explain or reproduce (e.g., particular cluster structures in compositional data).[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • What outcomes or summary statistics you will compare between model and data.

The model is always coupled to data:

  • “System theories” are built from empirical work (e.g., compositional analyses, cluster results).[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)
  • Simulation data and results are treated like experimental outcomes that are compared back to real observations.(Sargent 2013)

10.3 Worked Workflow

This section walks through a general simulation workflow, framed in terms of questions that arise in compositional and cluster-based archaeological analysis.

10.3.1 1. Define Objectives

Following the model development paradigm, begin with clear objectives.(Sargent 2013) Examples grounded in the cited work include:

  • Do simple grouping rules about production and exchange generate cluster structures comparable to those seen in Ward’s or average-link cluster analyses of Parowan Valley ceramics or PIXE-based ornament datasets?[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • Under what conditions do distinct compositional groups emerge, vs. when do they blur, as seen in the difficulties of establishing stable Mahalanobis-based group memberships?[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

Your objectives must specify:

  • The target patterns (e.g., dendrogram structure, distances, distribution of group membership probabilities).[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • How you will judge model performance.

10.3.2 2. Develop the Conceptual Model

Use your system theories to construct a conceptual model.(Sargent 2013) In an archaeological context, this might include:

  • Entities: sites, households, production units, or artifacts.
  • Processes: production, movement, exchange, discard.
  • Environment: geographic arrangement of sites, resource distributions (e.g., clays with different chemistries).
  • Time steps: yearly or generational cycles.

You can mirror analytic constructs already in use:

  • The presence of multiple compositional clusters, distinguished with Ward’s and average-link clustering, suggests multiple “sources” or “production traditions” in the conceptual model.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • The shifting Mahalanobis probabilities with different group configurations highlight the possibility of overlapping or poorly separated sources, or complex production behavior.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

You should specify:

  • Initial conditions (e.g., number of sources or sites).
  • Rules for how artifacts acquire composition (e.g., choice of clay source).
  • Rules for exchange or movement that will affect which compositions appear at which sites.

10.3.3 3. Specify the Simulation Model

Write a simulation model specification that details how the conceptual model will be implemented.(Sargent 2013) This includes:

  • Data structures representing agents or sites.
  • Algorithms for production and exchange processes.
  • How you will record simulation outputs, so they can be analyzed with the same tools as the empirical data (e.g., principal components, cluster analysis, Mahalanobis distances).[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

The goal is to ensure that the computerized implementation is a faithful translation of the conceptual design.(Sargent 2013)

10.3.4 4. Implement and Verify

Implement the model in software. Then perform verification: check that the computerized model correctly implements the intended conceptual model.(Sargent 2013) This is about correctness of code, not about match to data.

Verification steps may include:

  • Simplified test scenarios where you know the expected behavior (e.g., a single source should yield a single tight compositional group in clustering).
  • Internal consistency checks on model outputs (e.g., group counts, distributions).

10.3.5 5. Validate Against Data

Conceptual model validation and operational validation ask whether the model structure and its outputs are adequate representations of the real system for the study’s objectives.(Sargent 2013)

In an archaeological context:

  • Run the simulation to generate synthetic artifacts and their “compositions.”

  • Apply the same analyses used in the empirical studies: PCA plots, Ward’s and average-link cluster analyses, Mahalanobis distance-based group assignment.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

  • Compare:

    • Number and separation of clusters in dendrograms.
    • Distribution of distances and probabilities of group membership.
    • Sensitivity of these metrics to changes in model assumptions.

If your simulated clusters show the same instabilities in Mahalanobis probabilities observed in real data when groups are tightened—where previously high-probability samples become low-probability members in revised group configurations—that suggests your model captures similar complexities in group structure or data quality.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

If not, your system theories and conceptual model may need revision.(Sargent 2013)

10.4 Case Study: Grouping Parowan Valley Ceramics

10.4.1 Empirical Pattern

Studies of Parowan Valley ceramics apply multivariate analyses (including principal components) and hierarchical clustering (Ward’s algorithm and average-link) to identify compositional groups.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006) The analyses reveal:

  • Multiple cases (samples) that cluster into groups based on chemical composition, as shown in dendrograms with distance scales.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • When groups suggested by cluster analysis are evaluated with Mahalanobis distance, initial results are promising, but after removing samples with low group membership probabilities (P < 0.1) and recalculating, probabilities for remaining samples often fail to increase, and some samples with previously high probabilities now show extremely low probabilities in the “tighter” groups.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

Several explanations are proposed for these instabilities, including issues with sample digestion and the possibility of obscured compositional groups.(Watkins and Janetski 2006)

10.4.2 Simulation Framing

A simulation model could be used to explore whether plausible production and exchange processes would naturally produce:

  • The observed number and structure of clusters under Ward’s and average-linkage algorithms.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • The counterintuitive behavior of Mahalanobis-based group probabilities when clusters are redefined.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

Workflow:

  1. Objectives: Reproduce both the dendrogram structure and Mahalanobis probability behavior of Parowan Valley ceramic groups.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

  2. Conceptual Model: Define sources with different base chemistries and variable production practices; define households or workshops that choose sources under certain rules; model exchange between sites.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

  3. Specification: Plan to output “compositional” data matrices for simulated sherds that can be fed into the same PCA and cluster pipelines as the real data.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

  4. Verification: Check that, in controlled cases (e.g., single-source systems), clustering produces a single tight group.

  5. Validation: Examine whether multi-source, noisy models can:

    • Produce dendrograms similar in structure and distance scales to those reported.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
    • Generate Mahalanobis-based group probabilities that become unstable when group definitions are tightened, paralleling empirical findings.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)

Such a simulation does not “prove” any particular explanation, but it can test whether certain theoretical assumptions about production and exchange are consistent with the full pattern of clustering and group membership behavior.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

10.5 Common Mistakes

10.5.1 1. Vague or Shifting Objectives

Developing simulation models without clearly stated objectives violates the recommended model development paradigm, in which a simulation model should only be created for well-defined objectives.(Sargent 2013) Without precise questions (e.g., which aspects of group structure you are trying to reproduce), interpretation becomes ambiguous.

10.5.2 2. Ignoring the Conceptual–Computerized Distinction

Skipping directly to coding without a clear conceptual model can lead to implementations that do not meaningfully represent the system being studied.(Sargent 2013) This is especially problematic when later trying to diagnose why model outputs do or do not match data.

10.5.3 3. Treating Verification as Validation

Passing code-level checks (verification) does not mean the model is an adequate representation of the archaeological system (validation).(Sargent 2013) A model can be correctly implemented but based on inappropriate system theories.

10.5.4 4. Overinterpreting Cluster Outputs

Relying solely on cluster analysis outputs without careful evaluation of group strength and membership probabilities can be misleading. In the Parowan Valley ceramic study, even after pruning low-probability samples, recalculated Mahalanobis probabilities did not stabilize; some previously strong members became weak members of “tighter” groups.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006) Simulation outputs must be assessed with similar skepticism.

10.5.5 5. Neglecting Data Quality and Measurement Processes

In the ceramic case, one explanation for unstable grouping is that “near-total” digestion of samples was inadequate, possibly obscuring chemically distinct groups.(Watkins and Janetski 2006) Ignoring such measurement and preparation issues when designing or validating simulation models can lead you to adjust theoretical assumptions when the underlying problem lies in data quality.

10.6 Exercises

  1. Objective Formulation

    • Given a dataset of artifact compositions analyzed with Ward’s and average-link clustering, formulate two distinct simulation objectives that each specify:
      • The pattern to be reproduced (cluster structure, group membership probabilities).
      • The archaeological interpretation you hope to evaluate.
    • Explain how each objective constrains your conceptual model.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

  2. Conceptual Model Sketch

    • Based on the Parowan Valley ceramic case, sketch a conceptual model that could plausibly lead to the observed cluster structures and Mahalanobis behavior. Specify:
      • Entities, processes, and environment.
      • Which aspects explicitly reflect the explanations discussed for unstable group memberships.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

  3. Verification vs. Validation

    • Design three tests you would use for verification and three for validation of a ceramic compositional simulation model. For each test, briefly justify how it relates to:
      • Correct implementation vs. adequate representation of the archaeological system.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

  4. Data Quality Thought Experiment

    • Using the explanation that inadequate digestion may obscure distinct compositional groups,(Watkins and Janetski 2006) propose how you could incorporate measurement noise or sample preparation effects into a simulation model. How would you evaluate whether such noise could account for the behavior observed in Mahalanobis probabilities?[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)(Sargent 2013)

10.7 Key Takeaways

  • Simulation models in archaeology must be grounded in explicit system theories and built for well-defined objectives.(Sargent 2013)
  • The conceptual model is a mathematical, logical, or graphical representation of the system; the computerized model is its implementation in software.(Sargent 2013)
  • A rigorous workflow includes: defining objectives, building a conceptual model, specifying and implementing the simulation, verifying the implementation, and validating model behavior against data.(Sargent 2013)
  • Cluster analysis and Mahalanobis-based evaluation of group strength in ceramic and ornament datasets reveal complex, sometimes unstable group structures that any explanatory simulation must account for.[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Jardine 2007)[Jardine (2007)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)[Watkins and Janetski (2006)](Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • Data quality and measurement processes, such as incomplete digestion of ceramic samples, can significantly affect group identification and must be considered when constructing and validating models.(Watkins and Janetski 2006)
  • Verification (code correctness) and validation (model adequacy for the study’s goals) are distinct but equally necessary steps in trustworthy simulation-based inference.(Sargent 2013)

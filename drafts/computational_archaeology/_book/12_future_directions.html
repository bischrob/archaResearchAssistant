14.0.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Explain how trends in remote sensing, data fusion, and automation are reshaping archaeological research workflows.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  • Describe emerging needs in data standards, knowledge representation, and digital repositories for archaeological data reuse.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Critically assess infrastructure and organizational investments required for large‑scale, data‑intensive archaeology.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)
  • Identify skills and career pathways at the interface of computer science and archaeology, especially around data management and remote sensing.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

14.0.2 Core Ideas

14.0.2.1 12.1 Technological Trends and Research Frontiers

Remote sensing is moving toward increasingly advanced and integrated sensing systems that provide high‑resolution, highly detailed representations of archaeological sites.(Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023) Current trends include:

  • Integration of multiple sensors: Combining lidar, hyperspectral imaging, satellite imagery, aerial photography, ground‑penetrating radar (GPR), and SAR to collect a wide range of data in a single survey, enabling more comprehensive site understanding.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  • Data fusion as a research frontier:
    • Fusion of satellite, aerial, lidar, and GPR data can create detailed and accurate maps revealing buried structures or artifacts not visible in any single dataset.(Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
    • Combining hyperspectral data with lidar or SAR can provide 3D subsurface images, including information on shape, size, and depth of buried remains and soil chemistry indicative of archaeological features.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
    • Machine learning is used to analyze fused datasets to automatically identify and classify archaeological features, though this field is still emerging and methods are under active development.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  • Unmanned aerial systems (UAS): The use of unmanned aerial vehicles is an explicit trend, supporting flexible, high‑resolution data acquisition for remote sensing archaeology.(Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)

Remote sensing data fusion is recognized as complex, requiring deep understanding of sensor capabilities and limitations, and should be validated with ground-based survey and excavation rather than treated as conclusive on its own.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023) This combination of automation, fusion, and ground truthing defines a major frontier in computational archaeology.

14.0.2.2 12.2 Data Reuse, Standards, and Knowledge Infrastructure

Future computational archaeology depends on robust data reuse, which in turn requires improvements at every stage of data management—from creation through dissemination and archiving.(Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

Key challenges and directions:

  • Data quality at creation time:
    • Errors in coded data and mismatches between coding documentation and actual data greatly increase the effort required for reuse.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Poor data modeling, such as forcing complex phenomena into oversimplified flat tables, can impede later reuse and analysis.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Good practice includes explicit validation, data typing, and choice of open, nonproprietary file formats, as part of a broader suite of data management techniques.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Identifiers and relational thinking:
    • Identifier practices are fundamental to shaping data quality and reusability, supporting more relational and contextual perspectives on archaeological datasets rather than treating them as isolated works.(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Experience reusing others’ data helps researchers recognize “good data,” feeding back into improved data creation and “virtuous cycles” of better quality and higher‑impact reuse.(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Standards and contextual information:
    • Two classes of standards are needed for long‑term reuse: standards for research processes and standards for repositories that support discovery, manipulation, and integration.(Faniel et al. 2013)
    • In field archaeology, centralized curation and reuse are relatively new, robust field standards are limited, and existing ones are not widely followed.(Faniel et al. 2013)
    • Providing access to contextual information about how data were produced is seen as important for reuse, though much of the existing work on this comes from science and engineering rather than archaeology.[Faniel et al. (2013)](Faniel et al. 2013)

14.0.2.3 12.3 Repositories and Organizational Infrastructure

Digital publication in archaeology is shifting from books and monographs towards web‑based site reports and digital repositories.(Faniel et al. 2013) Infrastructure developments include:

  • Digital repositories and data services:
    • Systems such as Open Context provide web‑based data publication for cultural heritage and field research, and have been recognized by funding agencies (e.g., as a named venue for data deposit in data management plans).[Faniel et al. (2013)](Faniel et al. 2013)
    • Archaeology‑focused repositories and services (such as tDAR in the U.S., the Archaeology Data Service in the UK, and DANS in the Netherlands) support curation of digital archaeological data, including those produced by federal agencies.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
  • Knowledge structuring and management:
    • There is a recognized need for investments in data extraction, preservation, sharing, and reuse, including standards and formats for knowledge representation, ontologies, controlled vocabularies, and related tools.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
    • For some data classes, spatial metadata standards from organizations such as ISO and the Federal Geographic Data Committee (FGDC) are established enough that their use should be expected in professional work, and efforts are underway to develop cultural resource‑specific spatial metadata standards.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
    • Best practices in data collection and recording lead to substantial gains in data usability, supporting the value of investments in data infrastructure and training.(Kintigh et al. 2015)

These developments show that “doing science” is simultaneously about organizing science, transforming research culture, and enacting science policy, as seen in analogous efforts in ecology.(Kintigh et al. 2015) Computational archaeology is moving in the same direction, requiring coordinated community action and institutional support.

14.0.3 Worked Workflow: Data‑Intensive Remote Sensing Survey

This section sketches a future‑oriented, but grounded, workflow that integrates several of the trends outlined above.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)

  1. Project Design and Data Management Planning
    • Define research questions and specify what kinds of remote sensing (satellite, aerial, lidar, GPR, hyperspectral) and field data will be collected.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
    • Develop a data management plan that anticipates later reuse: choose open formats, define validation rules, and adopt identifier schemes to support relational linking among observations, locations, and finds.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  2. Data Acquisition
    • Conduct a multi‑sensor survey integrating lidar, hyperspectral imagery, and high‑resolution photography from unmanned aerial vehicles, and, where feasible, complement with GPR or SAR.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
    • Record detailed metadata following spatial metadata standards where applicable, and emerging cultural resource‑specific metadata schemes where available.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
  3. Data Fusion and Analysis
    • Align and fuse datasets to produce composite 3D and multispectral representations of the survey area, aiming to detect buried structures and soil anomalies suggestive of archaeological remains.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
    • Apply machine learning models to the fused data to automatically identify and classify candidate features, while explicitly treating these outputs as hypotheses.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  4. Ground Verification and Iteration
    • Use ground-based survey and targeted excavation to evaluate machine‑identified features and refine models, recognizing that data fusion alone is not conclusive.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  5. Data Publication and Curation
    • Clean and model the data to align with community standards and best practices, minimizing coding errors and ambiguous representations.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Deposit datasets, codebooks, and documentation (including contextual information about data collection and processing) in a disciplinary repository such as Open Context or other archaeological data services.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Faniel et al. 2013)
    • Use persistent, well‑managed identifiers within and across datasets to facilitate future linking and integration.(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  6. Reuse and Synthesis
    • Other teams can reuse these datasets in new syntheses, potentially combining them with additional repository holdings using shared metadata standards, ontologies, and identifiers.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Experiences from reuse feed back to refine data models and identifier practices for subsequent projects, reinforcing better practice across the community.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

This workflow exemplifies how computational archaeology is likely to operate: highly technical, data‑intensive, repository‑centered, and iterative between automated analysis and field verification.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

14.0.4 Case Study: Building an Integrated Knowledge Infrastructure

Consider a hypothetical national program aiming to support large‑scale research on cultural dynamics over long timescales. The program draws on lessons from ecology, where investments in organizations such as NCEAS, as well as archaeological and broader humanities repositories, have helped build collaborative research cultures.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kintigh et al. 2015)

Key elements of such an initiative in archaeology, grounded in existing recommendations, would include:

  1. Repository Network and Services
    • Expand or interconnect services like tDAR, Archaeology Data Service, DANS, and Open Context to curate digital archaeological data from government agencies and research projects.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Faniel et al. 2013)
    • Provide tools for data extraction, preservation, and sharing, backed by sustained investment.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
  2. Standards, Ontologies, and Controlled Vocabularies
    • Develop and adopt domain ontologies and controlled vocabularies to support consistent knowledge representation and cross‑project synthesis.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
    • Leverage well‑established spatial metadata standards, and participate in efforts (such as those under the FGDC umbrella) to create cultural resource‑specific standards.(Kintigh et al. 2015)
  3. Training and Practice Change
    • Train archaeologists and collaborators in data modeling, validation, identifier practices, and repository use, emphasizing that aligning data creation with reuse needs reduces downstream cleanup.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Encourage researchers to gain experience as data reusers to better understand quality expectations, thereby initiating “virtuous cycles” of improved data creation and reuse.(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  4. Policy and Incentives
    • Align funding requirements, such as data management plans and deposit mandates, with repository capabilities and community standards.[Faniel et al. (2013)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kintigh et al. 2015)
    • Recognize data creation, curation, and reuse as valued professional outputs to motivate adherence to best practices.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kintigh et al. 2015)

This case highlights that future computational archaeology depends not only on algorithms and sensors, but also on coordinated institutional arrangements, policies, and shared infrastructures.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

14.0.5 Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

As computational methods spread in archaeology, several recurring errors can undermine long‑term value.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

  1. Treating Data Fusion Outputs as Ground Truth
    • Over‑relying on fused remote sensing products or machine learning classifications without ground verification, despite explicit cautions that data fusion is not always conclusive and should be complemented by survey and excavation.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  2. Neglecting Data Modeling and Validation
    • Encoding complex archaeological phenomena into oversimplified tables without appropriate modeling, which can impede reuse.(Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
    • Failing to validate coded data or maintain accurate coding documentation, leading to hidden errors and expensive cleanup.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  3. Ignoring Identifiers and Relational Structure
    • Treating datasets as isolated files rather than as part of relational, contextual webs, and neglecting identifier practices that enable cross‑dataset linkage.(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  4. Under‑documenting Context of Production
    • Omitting detailed methodological context and provenance information needed for others to interpret and reuse the data, despite evidence that contextual information is critical for reuse and that standards must account for it.[Faniel et al. (2013)](Faniel et al. 2013)
  5. Fragmented or Non‑standard Metadata
    • Using ad hoc or proprietary metadata structures instead of established and emerging standards, thereby limiting discoverability and integration.[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)
  6. Treating Repositories as Afterthoughts
    • Waiting until the end of a project to adapt complex data for deposit, incurring significant late‑stage cleanup costs that could have been reduced by early alignment with repository requirements.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)

Avoiding these pitfalls requires integrating concerns about reuse, standards, and infrastructure from the outset of computational projects.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)

14.0.6 Exercises

  1. Design a Data Fusion Plan
    • For a hypothetical landscape survey combining lidar, hyperspectral imagery, and UAV photography, sketch a plan for data fusion and automated feature detection.
    • Identify at least three specific risks or limitations in the fusion and classification process and explain how you would use ground-based methods to address them, referencing the need for verification.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  2. Critique a Data Model
    • Given a simple flat table representing a complex archaeological phenomenon (e.g., multiple attributes of a site or artifact), identify at least three ways in which the model might impede reuse, drawing on issues raised about poor data modeling and coded data.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Propose improvements that would enhance future reuse (e.g., normalization, explicit coding documentation, data typing).[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  3. Repository‑Ready Dataset Checklist
    • Create a checklist for preparing a dataset for deposit in an archaeological repository, covering metadata standards, identifiers, contextual documentation, and file formats.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Explain how each item on your checklist contributes to data discoverability, integration, or long‑term reuse.[Faniel et al. (2013)](Kintigh et al. 2015)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  4. Career Path Mapping
    • Outline a possible career trajectory for a computer science graduate entering computational archaeology focused on remote sensing and data repositories.
    • Identify technical skills (e.g., machine learning, remote sensing, metadata standards, ontology design, data validation) and institutional contexts (e.g., repositories, research centers, government agencies) that this path would likely involve, based on the roles and infrastructures described in the readings.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)

14.0.7 Key Takeaways

  • Computational archaeology is moving toward sophisticated, multi‑sensor remote sensing and data fusion, often analyzed with machine learning, but these methods must be combined with ground‑based validation.[Agapiou and Lysandrou (2023)](Agapiou and Lysandrou 2023)
  • Long‑term value lies in data reuse, which depends on early‑stage data modeling, validation, and documentation practices that anticipate future users.[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Identifiers, ontologies, controlled vocabularies, and metadata standards are central to building interoperable archaeological knowledge infrastructures.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Digital repositories and data services, such as Open Context and archaeological data archives, are becoming core components of the research ecosystem and are recognized by funders and regulators as key venues for data curation.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Faniel et al. 2013)
  • Community‑level investments in standards, repositories, and training mirror successful initiatives in other fields and are necessary to support deep, data‑intensive research on cultural dynamics and deep time.[Kintigh et al. (2015)](Kintigh et al. 2015)
  • Future careers in computational archaeology will sit at the intersection of technical expertise (remote sensing, data science, knowledge representation) and stewardship of shared data infrastructures and standards.[Kintigh et al.

5 3. Programming and Reproducible Workflows

5.0.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Explain why reproducible computational workflows matter for archaeological data reuse and quality. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Describe how version control supports incremental improvement and transparent change tracking in research data. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Distinguish between ad hoc data handling (e.g., spreadsheets) and structured, well‑modeled workflows and databases. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Identify validation and data modeling practices that reduce downstream cleanup costs and errors. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Relate reproducible workflows to broader principles for data reuse (e.g., FAIR) and community standards. [Kansa and Kansa (2022)](Faniel et al. 2013)(Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.2 Core Ideas

5.0.2.1 3.1 Why Reproducible Workflows in Archaeology?

Digital archaeological data increasingly move into web‑based repositories and publication venues, rather than being confined to printed monographs and reports. (Faniel et al. 2013) New digital repositories have been created specifically to publish field and cultural heritage data online. (Faniel et al. 2013) Policies such as funder‑mandated data management plans and open access expectations highlight growing attention to research data as a key output of archaeological work. [Faniel et al. (2013)](Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

However, data are often difficult to reuse because of problems that arise from the very beginning of data creation through later dissemination and archiving. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) Reuse can require significant editorial review, revision, annotation, and cleanup by data editors with domain expertise. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022) When data are hard to interpret, inconsistently coded, or poorly structured, downstream users face high costs and may struggle to integrate them into new analyses. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Faniel et al. 2013)

Reproducible computational workflows—built around programmatic tools, transparent procedures, and documented decisions—directly address these problems by:

  • Making it easier to track how data were processed and analyzed over time. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Encouraging good data modeling and validation practices at the point of creation. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Supporting integration and interoperability with other datasets and tools, which aligns with findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable (FAIR) principles. (Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.2.2 3.2 Version Control and Incremental Improvement

Traditional publishing is oriented toward fixed, final products, but data and analyses often benefit from continual and incremental improvements. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) Data publishing in archaeology can explicitly take advantage of version control systems to support this ongoing refinement. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

Using version control in a research workflow enables:

  • Incremental updates to data and code, instead of one‑off, opaque revisions. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • A recorded history of changes that can be reviewed by collaborators or future reusers. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Better alignment with emerging guidance on data management required by major funders, which increasingly expect explicit planning for how data will be curated and updated. [Faniel et al. (2013)](Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

In a reproducible archaeological workflow, scripts, notebooks, and even data models can be tracked under version control so that each change to cleaning rules, coding schemes, or analysis steps is documented and reversible. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

5.0.2.3 3.3 Scripts, Notebooks, and Data Pipelines

Many archaeologists still collect and manage data primarily using paper forms, customized spreadsheets, GIS projects, or ad hoc databases. (Ross et al. 2015) These approaches often lack a shared, reusable platform for exchanging methods, making data collection and transformation a “hit‑or‑miss, ad hoc affair.” (Ross et al. 2015)

Programmatic workflows using scripts and notebooks can turn this ad hoc handling into explicit data pipelines that:

  • Read raw field or laboratory data from structured sources. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Apply cleaning, validation, and reformatting operations. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Output well‑formed relational data that are granular, non‑redundant, and robust to later integration. (Ross et al. 2015)

Relational database management systems (DBMSs) that separate the data “back end” (tables) from the “front end” (forms, reports, or other interfaces) help preserve data integrity and avoid accidental changes. (Ross et al. 2015) Well‑formed relational data are fine‑grained, regular, compact, and better suited for efficient analysis and sharing. (Ross et al. 2015) Scripts and pipelines that feed into such structures support reproducibility by ensuring that transformations from raw capture to relational tables are repeatable and inspectable. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)

5.0.2.4 3.4 Data Modeling and Validation

Poor data modeling and weak validation practices are major barriers to later data reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022) Modeling complex archaeological phenomena as overly simple, flat tables (e.g., spreadsheets) can impede reuse because important relationships and constraints are lost. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) Many researchers lack formal training in data management and modeling, which exacerbates these issues. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

Better practices, supported by programmatic workflows, include:

  • Explicitly modeling relationships (e.g., via linked tables in relational databases), which helps avoid redundancy and protect data integrity. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Enforcing data types (integers, decimals, Boolean values) to promote consistency and reduce errors. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Applying validation and decoding checks to catch mismatches between coded values and their documentation, which otherwise greatly multiplies the effort required for reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Using open and nonproprietary file formats to improve long‑term accessibility and interoperability. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

Implementing these constraints within scripts, databases, and pipelines prevents many errors at the point of data entry and transformation, rather than leaving them to be discovered during later reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)

5.0.2.5 3.5 Reuse, FAIR, and Community Standards

Data reuse is increasingly recognized as a professional goal that motivates better data management practices throughout the data lifecycle, from creation through archiving. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Faniel et al. 2013) Experience reusing others’ data helps researchers develop clearer expectations about what constitutes “good data,” and these expectations can feed back into improved data creation, forming “virtuous cycles” of better data practices and higher‑impact reuse. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

The FAIR framework articulates conditions that support such reuse:

  • Findable: data and metadata are registered or indexed in searchable resources. (Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Accessible: data and metadata can be retrieved by identifiers using open, standardized protocols, with authentication as needed. (Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Interoperable: data use formal, shared languages and vocabularies and include qualified references to other (meta)data so they can integrate with other workflows. (Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Reusable: data and metadata are richly described, licensed clearly, associated with detailed provenance, and aligned with domain‑relevant community standards. (Nicholson et al. 2023)

Reproducible computational workflows make it easier to document provenance (how data were generated and transformed) and to align with shared standards, both of which are central to reusability. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Nicholson et al. 2023) They also support the kinds of quality control and editorial services that modern data centers increasingly provide as part of online data publication. [Costello (2009)](Faniel et al. 2013)

5.0.3 Worked Workflow: From Field Data to Reusable Dataset

This section outlines a conceptual, stepwise workflow that illustrates how programming and reproducible practices can transform raw archaeological data into a reusable resource.

5.0.3.1 Step 1: Plan for Reuse at Data Creation

Because adapting complex data late in a project is costly, teams should align data creation practices with broader community reuse needs from the start. (Kansa and Kansa 2022) This planning includes:

  • Designing data models that reflect the complexity of archaeological observations rather than forcing them into oversimplified flat tables. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Anticipating specialized quality concerns relevant to particular data types (e.g., survey, geospatial, zooarchaeological, archaeobotanical), even though details vary across subfields. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Committing to open, nonproprietary formats and clear identifier practices that support a relational and contextual view of data. [Kansa and Kansa (2022)](Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.3.2 Step 2: Capture and Store Data in a Structured System

Instead of leaving data scattered across unstandardized spreadsheets, a well‑formed relational database is set up with:

  • Granular tables that avoid redundancy and sparseness. (Ross et al. 2015)
  • Explicit relationships between tables, which help preserve data integrity. (Ross et al. 2015)
  • A back‑end / front‑end separation, where forms and reports do not directly alter the underlying data structures. (Ross et al. 2015)

Scripts or controlled forms feed field and lab observations into this database, applying immediate checks for required fields, valid codes, and appropriate data types. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)

5.0.3.3 Step 3: Implement Programmatic Validation and Decoding

Next, programmatic routines (e.g., scripts) are used to validate the database contents:

  • Coded fields are decoded and cross‑checked against their documentation to identify mismatches, which are otherwise hard to detect and greatly increase reuse effort. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Data types are enforced so that integers, decimals, and Boolean values appear where expected, reducing inconsistency and error rates. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Additional checks ensure regularity and compactness of the relational structure. (Ross et al. 2015)

These validation scripts can be rerun whenever new data are entered, forming part of a repeatable pipeline. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)

5.0.3.4 Step 4: Track Changes with Version Control

All database schemas, validation scripts, and transformation notebooks are stored under version control, allowing:

  • Continuous incremental improvements to data management rather than a single, frozen version. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Transparent documentation of how validation rules, code lists, and data models evolve over time. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Easier collaboration around data publishing practices that support integration and analysis. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

This versioned record contributes to the provenance information needed for future data reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.3.5 Step 5: Prepare Data and Metadata for Publication

Before deposit in a digital repository, the team:

  • Exports data to open, nonproprietary formats. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Assembles rich metadata, including detailed attributes, usage licenses, and provenance. [Kansa and Kansa (2022)](Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Aligns terminology and structure with community standards where available. [Kansa and Kansa (2022)](Nicholson et al. 2023)

Repositories and data centers can then provide additional quality control and online publication services, adding value through their editorial role. [Costello (2009)](Faniel et al. 2013) The resulting dataset is more findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable. (Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.4 Case Study: Data Editors, Cleanup, and the Cost of Weak Workflows

Collaborative archaeological studies that integrate data from multiple projects reveal the impact of initial data management choices on later reuse.

In one such context, data intended for collaborative analysis had to pass through intensive editorial review, revision, and annotation before they could be effectively reused. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) This process required significant effort and domain expertise from dedicated “data editors.” (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) Several key issues emerged:

  • Errors in coded data were difficult to detect, especially where coding documentation did not precisely match the coded values in the dataset. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Data stored in coded form, even when some documentation existed, greatly multiplied the effort needed for reuse because codes had to be carefully decoded and reconciled. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Poor data modeling, particularly attempts to capture complex archaeological phenomena in overly simple flat tables, made it harder to reinterpret or recombine data for new questions. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

Subsequent work on data quality and reuse emphasizes that adapting complex data “late in the game” is costly, and many of these costs could be reduced if projects aligned data creation practices with wider community reuse needs from the outset. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

The experience of acting as data reusers in such projects helps researchers develop clearer expectations about “good data” and, in turn, become better data creators. (Kansa and Kansa 2022) This feedback loop can help establish virtuous cycles where improved data creation leads to more and better reuses, which further reinforces good practice. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

Reproducible computational workflows—featuring explicit data models, validation checks, and version‑controlled scripts—directly respond to the challenges observed in these collaborative integrations. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Ross et al. 2015)

5.0.5 Common Mistakes

Common pitfalls in programming and reproducible workflows for computational archaeology include:

  1. Treating spreadsheets as final databases
    Relying on flat spreadsheets to represent complex archaeological phenomena can obscure relationships and constraints, making reuse and integration difficult. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) Well‑formed relational data are better suited for clean, reusable structures. (Ross et al. 2015)

  2. Deferring data modeling decisions until late in a project
    Many data modeling problems only become apparent during reuse, when they are expensive to fix. (Kansa and Kansa 2022) Late adaptation of complex data leads to significant cleanup and translation work. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

  3. Using opaque codes without rigorous documentation and validation
    Coded data can hide errors, especially when codebooks do not match the actual content. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014) This discrepancy greatly multiplies the effort required for reuse. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

  4. Ignoring validation and data type enforcement
    Failing to specify and enforce appropriate data types (integer, decimal, Boolean, etc.) encourages inconsistent entries and errors that compromise data quality. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

  5. Relying on proprietary formats and closed tools
    When data are locked into proprietary formats, long‑term accessibility and interoperability suffer. (Kansa and Kansa 2022) Open, nonproprietary formats are more suitable for sharing and reuse. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

  6. Treating data as static, final products
    Approaching data like traditional publications overlooks the benefits of ongoing, version‑controlled refinement, which is central to robust data publishing. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)

  7. Under‑documenting provenance and context
    Without detailed provenance and contextual information, future reusers struggle to understand how data were created and processed, undermining reusability. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)[Faniel et al. (2013)](Nicholson et al. 2023)

5.0.6 Exercises

  1. Diagnose a Flat Table
    You are given a single, large spreadsheet that attempts to capture all information from an excavation, including artifacts, stratigraphic units, and spatial coordinates in one table.
    • Identify at least three potential data modeling problems that could impede reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Ross et al. 2015)
    • Propose a relational redesign (list of tables and relationships) that would improve granularity, reduce redundancy, and protect data integrity. (Ross et al. 2015)
  2. Validation Rules Design
    For a zooarchaeological dataset with coded taxonomic and anatomical fields:
    • List specific validation checks you would implement to ensure that coded values match their documentation. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
    • Describe how you would enforce data types for key quantitative measurements (e.g., lengths, weights). (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  3. Versioning a Data Pipeline
    Imagine you have a script that cleans and transforms survey data for publication in an online repository.
    • Describe how you would use version control to manage ongoing improvements to this script and to the underlying data model. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
    • Explain how the version history contributes to data provenance and reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)[Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Nicholson et al. 2023)
  4. Aligning with FAIR
    Choose a hypothetical archaeological dataset (e.g., an archaeobotanical assemblage). For each FAIR component, specify one concrete action you would take to improve:
    • Findability (e.g., indexing or registration). (Nicholson et al. 2023)
    • Accessibility (e.g., protocols, metadata availability). (Nicholson et al. 2023)
    • Interoperability (e.g., shared vocabularies, references). (Nicholson et al. 2023)
    • Reusability (e.g., license, provenance, community standards). (Nicholson et al. 2023)
  5. Reflecting on Reuse Experience
    Assume you have just attempted to reuse a legacy dataset that lacked clear codebooks and had ambiguous tabular structures.
    • Identify at least three difficulties you encountered. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Faniel et al. 2013)
    • For each, state a concrete change you would make in your own future data creation practices to avoid imposing similar burdens on others. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)

5.0.7 Key Takeaways

  • Reproducible computational workflows are crucial in archaeology because data move through a complex lifecycle from creation to dissemination and reuse, and poor practices at early stages lead to costly cleanup later. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Faniel et al. 2013)
  • Version control supports continual, incremental improvement of datasets and code, aligning data publishing with ongoing refinement rather than fixed final products. (Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle 2014)
  • Well‑modeled relational data, supported by DBMSs that separate back end and front end, yield granular, non‑redundant, and robust structures better suited to analysis and sharing than ad hoc spreadsheets. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Ross et al. 2015)
  • Data validation, decoding of codes, and enforcement of data types are essential practices that reduce errors and significantly lower the effort required for data reuse. [Kansa, Kansa, and Arbuckle (2014)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)
  • Open, nonproprietary formats and rich metadata—including licenses, provenance, and alignment with community standards—are central to making data findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable. [Costello (2009)](Kansa and Kansa 2022)(Nicholson et al. 2023)
  • Experience reusing other researchers’ data informs better data creation and can establish virtuous cycles where high‑quality, well‑documented datasets lead to more impactful reuse and improved community norms. (Kansa and Kansa 2022)
